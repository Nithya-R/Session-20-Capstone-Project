# The Union Parliament: India's Supreme Law-Making Body

Welcome to Level 5 Civics! Today, we will explore the **Union Parliament (Sansad)**, the heart of India's law-making process and a cornerstone of its democracy.

## 1. What is Parliament?

The Union Parliament, also known as the **Sansad**, is India's highest law-making institution. It's where representatives chosen by the people discuss, debate, and create the laws that govern our nation. In a parliamentary system like India's, the government is directly accountable to the Parliament.

The Parliament is composed of three key parts:
*   **The President of India**: The head of state.
*   **The Lok Sabha (House of the People)**: The lower house.
*   **The Rajya Sabha (Council of States)**: The upper house.

--- 

## 2. The Lok Sabha: The House of the People

The Lok Sabha is where the voice of the Indian citizens is directly heard. Its members are elected by the people themselves.

*   **Members**: It can have up to 543 elected members, each representing a specific geographical area (constituency).
*   **Elections**: Members are chosen through **Universal Adult Franchise**, meaning every Indian citizen aged 18 or above has the right to vote.
*   **Term**: Members serve for a term of **5 years**, though the house can be dissolved sooner under certain circumstances.
*   **Leader**: The **Speaker** presides over the Lok Sabha and is elected from among its members.

--- 

## 3. The Rajya Sabha: The Council of States

The Rajya Sabha acts as a voice for the states and union territories within the Indian federation.

*   **Members**: It has 245 members. Most (233) are indirectly elected by the elected members of the State Legislative Assemblies.
*   **Nomination**: The President of India has the power to nominate **12 members** who have special knowledge or practical experience in fields like Art, Literature, Science, or Social Service.
*   **Nature**: The Rajya Sabha is a **permanent body** and is not subject to dissolution. Members serve a **6-year term**, with one-third of the members retiring every two years, ensuring continuity.
*   **Presiding Officer**: The **Vice-President of India** serves as the ex-officio Chairman of the Rajya Sabha.

--- 

## 4. Key Functions of Parliament

Parliament plays several crucial roles:

### 1. Law-Making

This is Parliament's primary function. It debates and approves proposed laws, called **Bills**. Once a Bill is passed by both the Lok Sabha and the Rajya Sabha, and receives the President's assent, it becomes an **Act** (a law).

### 2. Financial Control

Parliament has the power to control the government's money. The **Union Budget**, which outlines the government's income and expenditure, must be presented to and approved by Parliament. Importantly, **Money Bills** (dealing with finances) can only be introduced in the Lok Sabha.

### 3. Controlling the Executive

Parliament acts as a watchdog over the government (the Executive). It keeps the government in check through various mechanisms:
*   **Question Hour**: Members can ask questions to ministers about government policies and actions.
*   **Zero Hour**: A period for raising urgent matters.
*   **Debates**: Discussions on important issues.

If the government loses the confidence of the Lok Sabha, it can be forced to resign through a **"No-Confidence Motion."**

--- 

## 5. How Laws Are Made: The Legislative Process

Here's a simplified overview of how a Bill becomes a Law:

1.  **Introduction**: A Bill is first introduced in either the Lok Sabha or the Rajya Sabha (except for Money Bills, which start in the Lok Sabha).
2.  **Readings**: The Bill goes through several stages, including detailed discussions, debates, and clause-by-clause examination (known as readings) in the originating house.
3.  **Approval by the Second House**: After being passed by the first house, the Bill is sent to the other house, where it undergoes a similar process of discussion and approval.
4.  **Presidential Assent**: Once both houses approve the Bill, it is sent to the President of India. The President's signature makes the Bill a formal Law (Act) of the land.
