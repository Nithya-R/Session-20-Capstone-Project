# Level 14 Civics – Emergency Provisions and Constitutional Amendments

*(How the Constitution handles crises and adapts to change)*

## 1. What are Emergency Provisions?

The Indian Constitution contains special provisions (Part XVIII) to handle extraordinary situations where the security, stability, or financial health of the country is at risk. During an emergency, the federal structure turns into a unitary one, giving more power to the Central Government.

---

## 2. Three Types of Emergencies

### National Emergency (Article 352)
- **Declared by**: The President in case of war, external aggression, or armed rebellion.
- **Impact**: The Centre can give directions to states on any matter, and fundamental rights (except life and liberty) can be suspended.

### State Emergency / President's Rule (Article 356)
- **Declared when**: A state government cannot function according to the Constitution.
- **Impact**: The Governor runs the state on behalf of the President, and the State Assembly is often suspended.

### Financial Emergency (Article 360)
- **Declared if**: The financial stability of India is threatened.
- **Note**: This has **never** been declared in India so far.

---

## 3. Amending the Constitution (Article 368)

A constitution must be a "living document"—it needs to change with the needs of society. The process of making these changes is called an **Amendment**.

**Who can amend?**
- Only the Parliament has the power to amend the Constitution. State legislatures cannot initiate an amendment.

---

## 4. Types of Amendment Procedures

The Constitution provides three ways to amend:
1. **Simple Majority**: Changes like forming new states or changing names of states. This is relatively easy.
2. **Special Majority**: Requires 2/3rd members of each House to vote in favor. Most amendments follow this.
3. **Special Majority + State Ratification**: For changes affecting the federal structure (e.g., electing the President), at least half of the State Legislatures must also agree.

---

## 5. The "Basic Structure" Doctrine

In 1973 (Kesavananda Bharati case), the Supreme Court ruled that while Parliament can amend the Constitution, it **cannot** change its "Basic Structure." Principles like secularism, federalism, democracy, and independence of the judiciary are protected and cannot be removed even by an amendment.

---

## 6. Key Ideas from Level 14 Civics

After completing this level, learners should understand:
- The purpose of emergency provisions in a democracy
- The three types of emergencies (National, State, Financial)
- Why the Constitution is called a "living document"
- The different levels of difficulty in the amendment process
- The significance of the "Basic Structure" doctrine in protecting democracy
